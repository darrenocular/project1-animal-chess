斗兽棋 (Animal Chess)

Rules

Introduction

Welcome to the jungle. This is a game of chess, played with opposing factions of animals.

As with any society, high and low, there is a hierarchy. In this make-believe world, where mindless vying for power has descended upon its inhabitants, some semblance of normalcy (or so we think) has been given by God.

In order of power: Elephant, Lion, Tiger, Leopard, Wolf, Dog, Cat and Rat. So, the Elephant can "kill" another Elephant (and all other weaker animals), while the Lion can "kill" another Lion (and all other weaker animals) but not the Elephant, and so forth. Some have been blessed with size, while others haven't. Size matters.

With perhaps the exception of the Rat. As an old Chinese legend goes, the Rat has the ability to scurry into the Elephant's ear and gnaw at its brain. So, while the Elephant is the strongest in the field, the Elephant's Achilles' heel comes unfortunately in the form of the little one - the Rat. The Elephant cannot "kill" the Rat under any circumstances.

How to win?

Win by either moving any one of your animals into the opponent's den or wiping the jungle clean of all the opponent's animals.

Detailed gameplay

  • You may move a piece during every turn. All pieces can only move 1 square horizontally or vertically (not diagonally). However, note the special rules for the river below.
  • An animal cannot move into its own den.
  • All animals can move into either player's trap squares. An animal that enters any of the opponent's trap squares is reduced in rank to 0. It can be "killed" by any of the opponent's pieces (regardless of hierarchy) and does not have the power to "kill" any of the opponent's adjacent pieces. A trapped piece has its normal power restored when it exits the opponent's trap square.
  • There are special rules for the river: (1) only the Rat can enter the river; and (2) only the Lion and the Tiger can jump over a river to the next non-water square on the other side (and "kill" an opponent's animal of equal or lower rank). But a jumping move is blocked if there is a Rat of either side in the intervening water squares.
  • Because only the Rat can enter the river, a Rat in the river can only be killed by another Rat in the river.
  • The Rat can only "kill" the Elephant from land and not the river. This means that the Rat cannot move from the river to "kill" an Elephant on an adjacent non-water square.

It is the survival of the fittest. Will you take on the challenge?
